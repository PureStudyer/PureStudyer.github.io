Ankun Tian.

Hardware architect for computational biology.

I am a first-year M.Eng. candidate at the School of Integrated Circuits, Southeast University, advised by Prof. Ming Ling. My research lies at the intersection of computer architecture and computational biology, with a focus on domain-specific accelerators and multi-FPGA systems for large-scale molecular docking and diffusion-based structural biology workloads.

Broadly, I am interested in how the constraints of real silicon — on-chip memory hierarchies, cross-board communication, numerical precision — can be co-designed with scientific algorithms rather than simply accommodating them. I build what I study: RTL in SystemVerilog, CUDA kernels, and SystemC models, through to ASIC frontend sign-off.

Outside the lab I play jazz piano, shoot RAW on small cameras, and spend too many evenings listening to records through a ladder-DAC chain.

01.
Sep 2024 — Present
Lead Multi-FPGA SystemVerilog

Scalable Multi-FPGA Acceleration System for AutoDock Vina

Advisor: Prof. Ming Ling · School of Integrated Circuits, Southeast University

AutoDock Vina's energy grid is too large to fit on a single FPGA's BRAM, creating a hard on-chip memory wall for docking accelerators. This project introduces a distributed grid-storage architecture that partitions the grid across boards, connected by a cross-board long-ring pipeline running over high-speed serial links.

  • Designed the distributed storage scheme and ring-pipeline control logic in SystemVerilog, targeting a three-board Xilinx ZCU102 cluster.
  • Implemented the full RTL pipeline, cross-board flow control, and host-side orchestration; verified end-to-end on real hardware.
  • Achieved 29.9× speedup over a high-performance CPU baseline and 296.4× improvement in energy efficiency.
  • Work accepted as a conference paper at DATE 2026 (first author).
02.
Jun — Nov 2025
CUDA GPU Collab · VUB

High-Performance Optimisation of Uni-Dock2 on GPU

Advisors: Prof. Ming Ling (SEU) · Prof. Bruno da Silva (VUB Brussels)

A kernel-level performance study of Uni-Dock2, a large-scale CUDA molecular-docking engine. I profiled the CUDA execution in depth, restructured memory-access patterns and the parallel execution scheme, and refined the conformational-search step size to reduce unnecessary work during virtual screening.

  • Identified key memory-bandwidth and occupancy bottlenecks via Nsight-Compute profiling on NVIDIA datacentre GPUs.
  • Restructured CUDA kernels for coalesced global-memory access and improved warp-level parallelism.
  • Tuned search-algorithm hyperparameters to cut compute without affecting docking quality.
  • Achieved an average 1.91× end-to-end speedup on large-scale screening benchmarks.
03.
Sep 2023 — May 2024
SystemC ASIC Arch. Diffusion

Accelerator Architecture Design for DiffDock

Advisor: Prof. Ming Ling · School of Integrated Circuits, Southeast University

A hardware accelerator architecture tailored for DiffDock, a diffusion-model-based molecular docking framework. I identified the computational hotspots of the diffusion pipeline and designed the critical compute modules at the RTL level, with SystemC models supporting fast architectural exploration.

  • Profiled DiffDock to locate the dominant compute kernels under realistic input distributions.
  • Designed custom compute units and on-chip dataflow tailored to the graph / tensor workloads of diffusion-based docking.
  • Built SystemC behavioural models to evaluate design points before committing to RTL.
  • Achieved an average 1.67× speedup over an NVIDIA V100 GPU baseline while operating at just 200 MHz.

Conference Papers
[C1]

Breaking the BRAM Wall: Scalable Vina FPGA Acceleration via Distributed Grid Storage and Cross-Board Long-Ring Pipelines

Ankun Tian, Shuo Tang, Rui Chen, and Ming Ling*.

In Proceedings of the 2026 ACM/EDAC/IEEE Design, Automation & Test in Europe Conference & Exhibition (DATE '26), Verona, Italy, 2026. To appear.

Accepted DATE '26 CCF-B First Author Multi-FPGA Computational Biology
▸ Abstract ▾ Hide abstract

AutoDock Vina is a widely-used molecular docking engine whose energy-grid representation has long exceeded the BRAM capacity of a single FPGA, bounding the scalability of hardware accelerators. We present a distributed grid-storage architecture that partitions the energy grid across multiple FPGAs and connects them through a cross-board long-ring pipeline. Evaluated on a three-board ZCU102 cluster, the proposed system achieves 29.9× speedup over a high-performance CPU baseline and 296.4× improvement in energy efficiency, breaking the on-chip memory wall that has historically limited FPGA-based docking.

Note 01 · Mar 2026

On the warmth of ladder networks.

After a fortnight with a discrete R2R board in the main chain, the soundstage loosens — cymbals lose their digital glass edge and piano decays hang a little longer in the room. It isn't accurate in the lab sense, but it has a temperature that delta-sigma converters rarely find.

Paired lately with Bill Evans, Sunday at the Village Vanguard — an MFSL 45-rpm pressing, second side, around midnight with the lamps low.

Note 02 · Apr 2026

An hour before the city wakes.

Most Sundays the practice room is mine at seven. I work through the same three pages of Debussy's Arabesque No. 1, then spend the rest of the hour chasing a voicing out of Waltz for Debby — never quite catching it, which is rather the point.

The upright isn't a concert instrument, but its felt has softened the way old notebooks do.
